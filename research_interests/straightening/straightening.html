Row sort: For \( F \in F(\lambda, z) \), we define the row-sorting of \( F \), denoted \( \text{rowsort}(F) \), to be the filling that arises from \( F \) by reordering the values within the rows so that they increase weakly.

Let \( \lambda = (\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k) \) be a partition, and \( \zeta = (\zeta_1, \dots, \zeta_{\lambda_1}) \) its conjugate partition.

Let \( C(\lambda) \) be the group of sequences of permutations \( \underline{\pi} = (\pi_1, \dots , \pi_{\lambda_1} ) \), where each \( \pi_i \in S_{\zeta_i} \).

We define the rearrangement subset of \( C(\lambda) \) associated to \( F, S \in F(\lambda, z) \) to be the set:

\[ C_{F,S}(\lambda) = \{ \underline{\pi} \in C(\lambda) \mid F_{\underline{\pi}} \text{ and } S \text{ have the same row content} \}. \]

Definition 3.1.1. Let \( F, S \in F(\lambda, z) \). We define the rearrangement coefficient associated to these fillings to be:

\[ \mathcal{R}_{F,S} := \sum_{\pi \in C_{F,S}(\lambda)} \text{sgn}(\underline{\pi}) \]

Lemma 3.1.3. Let \( F, T, S \in F(\lambda, z) \). Let \( \sigma, \gamma, \sigma', \gamma' \) be fixed elements of \( C(\lambda) \). Suppose that for all \( \pi \in C_{T,S}(\lambda) \), we have \( \sigma \circ \pi \circ \gamma \in C_{F,S}(\lambda) \). Further, suppose for all \( \pi' \in C_{F,S}(\lambda) \), we have \( \sigma' \circ \pi' \circ \gamma' \in C_{T,S}(\lambda) \). Then:

\[ \mathcal{R}_{F,S} = \text{sgn}(\sigma) \cdot \text{sgn}(\gamma) \cdot \mathcal{R}_{T,S} = \text{sgn}(\sigma') \cdot \text{sgn}(\gamma') \cdot \mathcal{R}_{T,S}. \]

Lemma 3.1.5. Let \( T \in T(\lambda, z) \) and \( \pi \in C(\lambda) \) such that \( \pi \neq (\text{id}, \ldots, \text{id}) \). If \( S = \text{rowsort}(T_\pi) \), then \( S \succ \text{sort}(T) \).

Lemma. Suppose that there exists a basis \( \{s_1, \dots, s_n\} \). We can know that \(R_{s_i, s_j} = 0\) when \(i < j\).

Proof. Suppose that \(i < j\), then we can know that \(s_j \preceq s_i\) according to the definition. Then, we can list the entries of \(s_i\) and \(s_j\) as follows: \[ \begin{aligned} s_i &= [a_1, \dots, a_n] \\ s_j &= [b_1, \dots, b_n] \end{aligned} \] Then there exists \(k\leq n\) such that \(a_k > b_k\) and \(a_i = b_i\) for all \(i < k\) because of the definition. Since \(a_k\) and \(b_k\) should be located at the same square, we can know that they are on the same column. Suppose that the column is \(c\). We denote the entries of the column \(c\) of \(s_i\) as \(s_i^c\) and the entries of the column \(c\) of \(s_j\) as \(s_j^c\). We list all the entries of \(s_i^c\) and \(s_j^c\) as follows: \[ \begin{aligned} s_i^c &= [a_{i_1}, \dots, a_{i_k}] \\ s_j^c &= [b_{i_1}, \dots, b_{i_k}] \end{aligned} \]
