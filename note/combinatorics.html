Pigeon-hole Principle

\(\textbf{Theorem 1.1. (Pigeon-hole Principle).}\) Let \(A\) and \(B\) be two finite sets and \(f\) be a function from \(A\) to \(B\). If \(|A| > |B|\), then \(f\) is not one-to-one. (i.e. there exists \(a_1, a_2 \in A\) such that \(a_1 \neq a_2\) and \(f(a_1) = f(a_2)\)).

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)We will prove it with mathematical contrapositive. We need to show that if \(f\) is one-to-one, then \(|A| \leq |B|\). Suppose that \(f\) is one-to-one. Let \(A = \{a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\}\), which implies that \(|A| = n\). Then \(f(A) = \{f(a_1), f(a_2), \dots, f(a_n)\}\) and \(|f(A)| = n\). Therefore, \(A\subset B\). \[ \tag*{$\square$} \]

\(\textbf{Theorem 1.2. (Pigeon-hole Principle, general version).}\) Let \(A\) and \(B\) be two finite sets with \(|A| = n\) and \(|B| = m\). Let \(r\) be a positive integer and let \(f\) be a function from \(A\) to \(B\). If \(n > rm\), then there exists \(b \in B\) such that \[ |f^{-1}(b)| \geq r+1, \] where \(f^{-1}(b) = \{a \in A : f(a) = b\}\).

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)We will prove it with mathematical contrapositive. We need to show that if \(|f^{-1}(b)| \leq r\) for all \(b \in B\), then \(n \leq rm\). Suppose that \(|f^{-1}(b)| \leq r\) for all \(b \in B\). We define \[ S = \bigcup_{b \in B} f^{-1}(b). \] Therefore, \[ n = |A|\leq |S| = \sum_{b \in B} |f^{-1}(b)| \leq r\cdot m. \] Hence, we prove the theorem. \[ \tag*{$\square$} \]


Erdős-Szekeres theorem

\(\textbf{Theorem. }\)Given \(r, s\in\mathbb{N}\), any sequence of distinct real numbers with length at least \((r - 1)(s - 1) + 1\) contains a monotonically increasing subsequence of length \(r\) or a monotonically decreasing subsequence of length \(s\).

\(\textbf{Proof. }\) Given a sequence of length \( (r - 1)(s - 1) + 1 \), label each number \( n_i \) in the sequence with the pair \( (a_i, b_i) \), where \( a_i \) is the length of the longest monotonically increasing subsequence ending with \( n_i \) and \( b_i \) is the length of the longest monotonically decreasing subsequence ending with \( n_i \). (e.g. Let \(L = \{3, 2, 1, 5, 6, 7, 5, 11\}\). We can see that \(7\in L\) which has the longest monotonically increasing subsequence ending with \(7\): \(1, 5, 6, 7\), \(2, 5, 6, 7\), \(3, 4, 6, 7\). We can also see the longest monotonically decreasing subsequence ending with \(7\) is just \(7\) since every number before \(7\) on the sequence is less than \(7\). Thus, \(7\) has the pair \((4, 1)\).) Each two numbers in the sequence are labeled with a different pair: if \( i \lt j \) and \( n_i \leq n_j \) then \( a_i \lt a_j \), and on the other hand if \( n_i \geq n_j \) then \( b_i \lt b_j \). But there are only \( (r - 1)(s - 1) \) possible labels if \( a_i \) is at most \( r - 1 \) and \( b_i \) is at most \( s - 1 \), so by the pigeonhole principle there must exist a value of \( i \) for which \( a_i \) or \( b_i \) is outside this range. If \( a_i \) is out of range then \( n_i \) is part of an increasing sequence of length at least \( r \), and if \( b_i \) is out of range then \( n_i \) is part of a decreasing sequence of length at least \( s \).


Cardinality of power set of finite sets

\(\textbf{Theorem. }\)Let \(A\) be a finite set with \(|A| = n\). Then \(|P(A)| = 2^n\).

\(\textbf{Theorem. }\)Suppose that \(A = \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}) where \(n\in\mathbb{N}\). Inclusion and Exclusion

Given two finite sets \(A\) and \(B\), we define the union of \(A\) and \(B\) as \[ |A\cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A\cap B|. \]

\(\textbf{Theorem. }\)Given \(n\) subsets \(A_1, \dots, A_n\) of a finite set \(A\). Then \[ \left|\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right| = \sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{j-1} \sum_{1\leq i_1 < \cdots < i_j \leq n} \left|A_{i_1}\cap\cdots\cap A_{i_j}\right| = \sum_{J\subseteq[n]} (-1)^{|J|-1} \left|\bigcap_{j\in J} A_j\right|. \]

\(\textbf{Example. }\)How many numbers \(1\) to \(100\) are divisible by \(2\), \(3\), or \(5\)? We let \[ A_2 = \text{the set of numbers divisible by } 2, \] \[ A_3 = \text{the set of numbers divisible by } 3, \] \[ A_5 = \text{the set of numbers divisible by } 5. \] Then \(|A_i| = \lfloor 100/2\rfloor\). Thus, we can get \[ \begin{aligned} |A_2\cup A_3\cup A_5| &= |A_2| + |A_3| + |A_5| - |A_2\cap A_3| - |A_2\cap A_5| - |A_3\cap A_5| + |A_2\cap A_3\cap A_5| \\ &= \lfloor 100/2\rfloor + \lfloor 100/3\rfloor + \lfloor 100/5\rfloor - \lfloor 100/6\rfloor - \lfloor 100/10\rfloor - \lfloor 100/15\rfloor + \lfloor 100/30\rfloor \\ &= 50 + 33 + 20 - 16 - 10 - 6 + 3 \\ &= 74. \end{aligned} \]
