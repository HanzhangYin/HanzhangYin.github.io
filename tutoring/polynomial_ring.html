Definition

\(\textbf{Definition: }\)


Degree

For a polynomial \(f(x)\) in \(R[x]\), the degree of \(f(x)\) (i.e. \(\mathrm{deg}f(x)\)) is the largest power of \(x\) in \(f(x)\) with a non-zero coefficient.


Units of \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\)

A unit is an element in a ring which divides every element in the ring.

Generalization: \(\{\text{uits in \}R[x]\}=\{\text{units in \}R\}\)

This is true for \(R\) with property that \(R\) is an integral domain. (i.e. \(ab=0\Rightarrow a=0\) or \(b=0\)).

Question: What is the unit in \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}[x]\)?

Answer: When \(n\) is prime, the unit in \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}[x]\) is \((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times\)


Divisibility and the Division Algorithm

\(\textbf{Division Algorithm. }\)Suppose \(a(x)\) and \(b(x)\) are polynomials in \(R[x]\) with \(b(x)\neq 0\). Then there exist unique polynomials \(q(x)\) and \(r(x)\) in \(R[x]\) such that the leading coefficient of \(b(x)\) is a unit and \[ a(x)=b(x)q(x)+r(x) \] and either \(r(x)=0\) or \(\mathrm{deg}r(x)\lt\mathrm{deg}b(x)\).

\(\textbf{Note. }\)This is not alwasy correct.

\(\textbf{Example 1. }\)Divide \(x^3-7\) by \(x^2-2\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) with a quotient and remainder. \[ x^3-7=(x^2-2)(x)+(2x-7) \]

\(\textbf{Example 2. }\)Divide \(2x^2+x+1\) by \(2x\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[x]\) with a quotient and remainder. \[ 2x^2+x+1=2x\cdot (x)+(x+1) \] For this case, we can notice that \(\mathrm{deg}(x+1)=\mathrm{deg}(2x)\), so the Division Algorithm does not work.

\(\textbf{Note. }\)In particular, Division Algorithm holds when \(R\) is a field.

\(\textbf{Note. }\)In specific, Division Algorithm holds when the leading coefficient of \(b(x)\) is a unit.

Example of \(\mathrm{Z}/3\mathrm{Z}[x]/(x^2+1)\)

\[ \mathrm{Z}/3\mathrm{Z}[x]/(x^2+1) = \{0, 1, 2, x, x+1, x+2, 2x, 2x+1, 2x+2\}\cong\mathbb{Z}[i]/(3). \]

Is \(\mathbb{Z}[i]/(3)\) a field?

Suppose \(p\) is a rational prime such that \(a^2 + b^2 = 0\) in \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) impling \(a = b = 0\). Then \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}[i]\) is a field.

\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} is a field if and only if \(p\) is a prime.


Example of \(\mathbb{Z}[i]/(2+i)\)

\[ \mathbb{Z}[i]/(2+i) = \{0, i, 2i, 1+i, 1+2i\}\cong\{-2, -1, 0, 1, 2}\cong\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4}\cong\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}. \]


Division Algorithm in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\)

\(\textbf{Division Algorithm. }\)Suppose \(a\) and \(b\) are elements in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) with \(b\neq 0\). Then there exist unique elements \(q\) and \(r\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) such that \[ a=bq+r \] and either \(r=0\) or \(N(r)\lt N(b)\).

\(\textbf{Example 1. }\)Divide \(5+2i\) by \(1+2i\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) with a quotient, \(q\), and remainder, \(r\). \[ 5+2i=(1+2i)q+r \] And I want \(N(r)\lt N(1+2i)=5\). Since \(r = 5 + 2i - (1+2i)q\), we can get \[ N(r) = N(5 + 2i - (1+2i)q) \lt N(1+2i) = 5, \] or \[ \sqrt{N(r)} = \sqrt{N(5 + 2i - (1+2i)q)} \lt \sqrt{5}. \]

Division Algorithm holds when every complex number is \(\lt 1\) from an element of \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\).

\(\textbf{Reformulation. }\)FOr every \(x\in\mathbb{Z}[i]\), let \[ B_x = \{z\in\mathbb{C}, |z-x|\lt 1\}. \]


Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

\(\textbf{Theorem. }\)If \(f(x)\) is a non-constant polynomial in \(\mathbb{C}[x]\) with degree \(n\), then \(f(x)\) has a root in \(\mathbb{C}\). We can factor \(f(x)\) as \[ f(x)=a(x-c_1)(x-c_2)\cdots(x-c_n) \] where \(a\) is the leading coefficient of \(f(x)\) and \(c_1, c_2, \dots, c_n\) are the roots of \(f(x)\).


Root Theorem

\(\textbf{Theorem 1. }\)Let \(R\) be any ring. Suppose \(f(x)\in R[x]\) and \(f(c)=0\), where \(c\in R\). Then we can factor \(f(x)\) as \[ f(x)=(x-c)q(x) \] where \(q(x)\in R[x]\).

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)Since the coeffcient of \((x-c)\) is a unit, by the Division Algorithm, we can write \[ f(x)=(x-c)q(x)+r(x), \] where \(\mathrm{deg}r(x)\lt\mathrm{deg}(x-c)=1\). Since a polynomial of degree \(0\) is a constant, we have \(r(x)=r\), where \(r\in R\). Thus, we have \[ f(x)=(x-c)q(c)+r. \] Now, we plug in \(c\) into the equation, we have \[ 0=0\cdot q(c)+r = r. \] Therefore, we have \[ f(x)=(x-c)q(x)+0=(x-c)q(x). \]


\(\textbf{Theorem 2. }\)Let \(K\) be a field and \(f(x)\in K[x]\), where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are two roots of \(f(x)\). Then we can factor \(f(x)\) as \[ f(x)=(x-c_1)(x-c_2)q(x) \] where \(q(x)\in K[x]\).


\(\textbf{Theorem 3. }\)Let \(K\) be a field. Suppose \(c_1, \dots, c_m\in K) are distinct roots of \(f(x)\in K[x]\). Then we can factor \(f(x)\) as \[ f(x)=(x-c_1)(x-c_2)\cdots(x-c_m)q(x), \] where \(q(x)\in K[x]\).


\(\textbf{Corollary. }\)Let \(K\) be a field and \(f(x)\in K[x]\). Then \(f(x)\) has at most \(\mathrm{deg}f(x)\) roots in \(K\).


\(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is a Field

\(\textbf{Theorem . }\)Let \(p\) be a prime number in \(\mathbb{Z}\). Then \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is a field.

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)A field, \(K\), is a commutative ring with a multiplicative inverse for every non-zero element in \(K\). Since we know that \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is a commutative ring, we only need to show that every non-zero element in \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) has a multiplicative inverse. Let \(a\in\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) be a non-zero element. Then we have \(\gcd(a, p)=1\) for \(p\) is a prime number. According to Bezout's Identity, we can find \(x, y\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \[ ax+py=1. \] Thus, we have \[ ax=1-py. \] Hence, we have \(ax\equiv 1\pmod{p}\). According to Divison Algorithm, we can write \(x\) as \[ x=qp+r, \] where \(q, r\in\mathbb{Z}\) and \(0\leq r\lt p\). Thus, we have \[ ax=a(qp+r)=aqp+ar, \] and \[ aqp+ar\equiv 1\pmod{p}. \] Thus, we have \[ aqp + ar = 1 - py, \] \[ ar -1 = -py - aqp = p(-y-aq). \] Thus, we have \(ar\equiv 1\pmod{p}\). Therefore, for any non-zero element \(a\in\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\), we can find \(r\in\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) such that \(ar\equiv 1\pmod{p}\).

\(\textbf{Proposition 2. }\)Let \(p\) be a prime number, and \(d\mid p-1\). Then we have \[ x^d-1\mid x^{p-1}-1. \]

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)Since we know that \[ x^{p-1}-1 = (x-1)(x-2)\cdots(x-(p-1)), \] we can get \[ x^{d}-1\mid (x-1)(x_2)\cdots(x-(p-1)). \] Because of the unique factorization of \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}[x]\), we have \[ x^{d}-1 = (x-c_1)\cdots(x-c_d), \] where \(c_1, \dots, c_d\) are distinct in \((\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z})^\times\). The distinct number of roots of \(x^{d}-1\) in \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}\) is exactly \(d\).
