Group of Units

Group

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)A group is a set \(G\) together with a binary operation \(\cdot\) on \(G\) such that

  1. \(\cdot\) is associative;
    2. $$ (a\cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c) $$ for all \(a, b, c\) in \(G\).
  2. There is an identity \(1\) for \(\cdot\);
    3. $$ a \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot a = a $$ for all \(a\) in \(G\).
  3. Every element of \(G\) has an inverse.
    4. $$ a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = 1 $$ for all \(a\) in \(G\).

Ring

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)A ring is a set \(R\) together with two binary operations \(+\) and \(\cdot\) on \(R\) such that

  1. \(R\) is an abelian group under addition;
    2. $$ a+b = b+a $$ for all \(a, b\) in \(R\).
  2. \(\cdot\) is associative;
    3. $$ (a\cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c) $$ for all \(a, b, c\) in \(R\).
  3. \(\cdot\) is distributive over \(+\);
    4. $$ a \cdot (b + c) = a \cdot b + a \cdot c $$ and $$ (a + b) \cdot c = a \cdot c + b \cdot c $$ for all \(a, b, c\) in \(R\).
  4. There is an identity \(1 \neq 0\) for \(\cdot\);
    5. $$ a \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot a = a $$ for all \(a\) in \(R\).

Definition

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)Let \(R\) be a ring. Assume \(R\) has an identity \(1 \neq 0\). An element \(u\) of \(R\) is called a unit in \(R\) if there is some \(v\) in \(R\) such that \(uv = vu = 1\). The set of units in \(R\) is denoted \(R^\times\).


Group of Units

\(\textbf{Proposition. }\)Let \(R\) be a ring. Assume \(R\) has an identity \(1 \neq 0\). Then \(R^\times\) is a group under multiplication.

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)Firstly, \(1\) is in \(R^\times\) since \(1 \cdot 1 = 1\). Let \(u\in R^\times\) and we can know that there exists \(v\) such that $$ u\cdot v = v\cdot u = 1. $$ In other words, \(v\) is in \(R^\times\) as well. Thus, the inverse of \(u\) is in \(R^\times\). Therefore, \(R^\times\) is a group. \[ \tag*{$\square$} \]


Congruence class / residue class

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)Congruence class or residue class of \(a\;\text{mod }n\): $$ \bar{a}=\{a + kn\;|\;k\in\mathbb{Z}\}=\{\dots, a-n, a, a+n, a+2n, \dots}. $$


\((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times\)

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)The collection of residue classes of \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\): $$ \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}^\times = \{\bar{a}\in \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\; |\; \text{there exists }\bar{c} \in \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} with \bar{a}\cdot \bar{c}= \bar{1} (\text{i.e. }ac\equiv 1\;\text{mod }n)\}. $$

\(\textbf{Proposition 1. }\)\(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}^\times = \{\bar{a}\in\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\;|\;(a, n)=1\}\).

\(\textbf{Proof. }\) Let \(A:=\{\bar{a}\in\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\;|\;(a, n)=1\}\). Suppose \(a\in A\). Hence, we can know that \((a, n)=1\). According to \(\textbf{Corollary 1 of previous section}\) \(\textbf{Corollary 1. }\)If \(a\) and \(b\) are positive integers, \(b\neq 0\), then there exist integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that $$ ax+by=(a, b). $$ , we can get that $$ ax+ny = 1, $$ for some integers \(x, y\). Thus, we can know that $$ ax - 1 = -ny, $$ $$ ax\equiv 1\;\text{mod }n. $$ Thus, \(x\) can be the integer such that $$ \bar{a}\cdot \bar{x} = \bar 1, $$ and we can get \(A\subset \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}^\times\).
