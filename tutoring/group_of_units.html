Group of Units

Group

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)A group is a set \(G\) together with a binary operation \(\cdot\) on \(G\) such that

  1. \(\cdot\) is associative;
  2. There is an identity \(1\) for \(\cdot\);
  3. Every element of \(G\) has an inverse.

Ring

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)A ring is a set \(R\) together with two binary operations \(+\) and \(\cdot\) on \(R\) such that

  1. \(R\) is an abelian group under addition;
  2. \(\cdot\) is associative;
  3. \(\cdot\) is distributive over \(+\);
  4. There is an identity \(1 \neq 0\) for \(\cdot\).

Definition

\(\textbf{Definition. }\)Let \(R\) be a ring. Assume \(R\) has an identity \(1 \neq 0\). An element \(u\) of \(R\) is called a unit in \(R\) if there is some \(v\) in \(R\) such that \(uv = vu = 1\). The set of units in \(R\) is denoted \(R^\times\).


Group of Units

\(\textbf{Proposition. }\)Let \(R\) be a ring. Assume \(R\) has an identity \(1 \neq 0\). Then \(R^\times\) is a group under multiplication.

\(\textbf{Proof. }\)Firstly, \(1\) is in \(R^\times\) since \(1 \cdot 1 = 1\).
