Linear Approximation
\begin{definition}[Linear Approximation] Linear Approximation of \( \Delta f \) If \( f \) is differentiable at \( x = a \) and \( \Delta x \) is small, then \[ \Delta f \approx f'(a) \Delta x \] \end{definition}\pause \begin{remark} \(\Delta f \) is the actual change in \( f \) and \( f'(a) \Delta x \) is the estimated change. \end{remark} \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Inverse Trigonometric Derivatives} \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\sin^{-1} x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \] \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\tan^{-1} x) = \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \] \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\sec^{-1} x) = \frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \] \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\cos^{-1} x) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \] \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\cot^{-1} x) = -\frac{1}{1 + x^2} \] \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\csc^{-1} x) = -\frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \] \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Linearization} \begin{definition}[Linearization] To approximate the function \( f \) itself rather than the change \( \Delta f \), we use the linearization \( L(x) \) centered at \( x = a \), defined by \[ L(x) = f'(a)(x - a) + f(a) \] Notice that \( y = L(x) \) is the equation of the tangent line at \( x = a \). \end{definition} \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Extreme Values on an Interval} \begin{definition}[Extreme Values] Let \( f \) be a function on an interval \( I \) and let \( a \in I \). We say that \( f(a) \) is the \begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Absolute minimum} of \( f \) on \( I \) if \( f(a) \leq f(x) \) for all \( x \in I \). \item \textbf{Absolute maximum} of \( f \) on \( I \) if \( f(a) \geq f(x) \) for all \( x \in I \). \end{itemize} \end{definition} \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Existence of Extrema on a Closed Interval} A continuous function \( f \) on a closed (bounded) interval \( I = [a, b] \) takes on both a minimum and a maximum value on \( I \). \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Local Extrema} \begin{definition}[Local Extrema] We say that \( f(c) \) is a \begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Local minimum} occurring at \( x = c \) if \( f(c) \) is the minimum value of \( f \) on some open interval (in the domain of \( f \)) containing \( c \). \item \textbf{Local maximum} occurring at \( x = c \) if \( f(c) \) is the maximum value of \( f \) on some open interval (in the domain of \( f \)) containing \( c \). \end{itemize} \end{definition} \end{frame} \begin{frame} \frametitle{Critical Points} \begin{definition}[Critical Points] A number \( c \) in the domain of \( f \) is called a \textbf{critical point} if either \( f'(c) = 0 \) or \( f'(c) \) does not exist. \end{definition} \frametitle{Critical Points} \begin{theorem}[Critical Points] If \( f \) has a local maximum or minimum at \( x = c \), then \( c \) is a critical point of \( f \). \end{theorem} \end{frame}
